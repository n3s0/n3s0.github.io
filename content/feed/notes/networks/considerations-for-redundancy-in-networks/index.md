## What's All This About?

I thought I would provide some notes on personal and industry best practices for
networking. I hold personal standards for myself and the networks I manage.
Regardless of back and forth within the office or budget constraints. I tend to 
notice flaws in design and in some cases it keeps me up at night.

Both of which are valid reasons for not putting the items discussed into
practice. It doesn't mean that I wont speak up in these items regard when
they're noticed.

I have inherited networks that have good, bad, and ugly in my career. There is
no use in talking down on anyone elses design. So holding the discussion that
design is flawed is never a personal attack. It is just an opinion and an
invitation to consider improvement in future design.

I will not be pointing out any specific scenarios only providing advice out of
personal reflection on a few categories of redundancy. Along with pointing out
dependancies and pitfalls of certain claims that redundancy exists. When really
it exists in some. But, there is that one thing that will cause a fire over the
weekend.

> Reflection
> 
> We can only blame the previous parents of our network for so long. If at all.
> When a network is inherited it's pitfalls become your responsibility. It is
> now your job to inform stakeholders of the design flaws and provide remedy in
> a professional manner.
>
> No one is a scapegoat for something that is now your responsibility.

I will be discussing redundancy within personal standards using the following
topics. (I'll have to think about what the actual standards are)

- Circuit Redundancy
- Network Device Redundancy
- Power Redundancy
- Local Connectivity Redundancy

environment because hardware can fail at any time. Having one that hasn't failed
can add time to fix an issue that affects redundancy rather than service.
